Tactics

If they tell you not to look

These patterns appear in documented holder chats and marketing around Zenza, RYO, and COINHUB. Recognizing the script matters as much as checking the registry.

When someone says do not read this site

A request to ignore primary sources is itself a warning sign. Legitimate issuers welcome verification. If you are told a website, court record, or blockchain explorer is harassment, negativity, or not to be trusted, ask why looking is forbidden.

  • Do not trust me. Check EDGAR, houjin-bango, or Solscan yourself.
  • If the only answer is loyalty, not documents, that is the technique.
  • Your friend may repeat a script without knowing. The test is records, not friendship.

Your friend is not the enemy

Promoters inside groups often believe what they repeat. The same person may push manga one week and COINHUB media the next. Ask them to check one source with you. Side by side. Website on the left, registry on the right.

Skepticism inoculation

What you hear"People ask questions, but they will understand soon."

What it doesAdmits doubt exists, then dismisses it and promises future proof so you stop asking now.

CheckAsk for the document today, not the promise tomorrow.

Isolation from warners

What you hearCritics labeled negative, toxic, or troublemakers.

What it doesTrains the group to distrust anyone who brings registry or court records.

CheckRead the warning and verify it independently. Do not debate loyalty.

Trusted insider override

What you hearA friendly promoter says the company is fine. Evidence is ignored.

What it doesUses social trust to beat documents. Common in LINE groups.

CheckOne registry search. Does the filing match the website?

Narrative pivot

What you hearYesterday: read the manga. Today: like and spread COINHUB on X.

What it doesSame promoter, new script when the issuer needs a different story.

CheckTrack the message, not the person. What changed in 48 hours?

Legitimacy theater

What you hearSEC Compliant badges, karate logos, law firm names, Imperial Hotel events.

What it doesProduction value substitutes for registration. The stage looks real. The filing may not exist.

CheckSEC EDGAR company search. CertiK database. FSA list. One minute.

Coordinated amplification

What you hearInstructions to like, repost, and spread PR as if it were news.

What it doesTurns holders into a free PR army. Paid press can look organic.

CheckIs it journalism or a paid release? Who benefits from the likes?

Artificial urgency

What you hearBuy now. Rate increases tomorrow. Deadline tonight.

What it doesPressure before you can verify. Classic across TLC, bonds, and presales.

CheckPause. Run one verification. Urgency survives facts or it does not.

Jurisdiction fog

What you hearSingapore logo, Japan office, Nevada bankruptcy, Philippines team.

What it doesScatters attention across countries so no one checks the right registry.

CheckWhich entity issued your contract? Search that jurisdiction only.

Pre-emptive discreditation

What you hearDo not read that site. Do not trust court records. Only trust headquarters.

What it doesBlocks verification before you start. This page exists to name that move in advance.

CheckIf looking is forbidden, look anyway. Then decide from documents.

MLM recruitment chains

What you hearRecruit promoters from holders. Each recruiter brings in more. Tiers and rewards based on downline.

What it doesTurns investors into promoters. The recruiter is paid in tokens or referral fees for bringing in new money. The recruiter becomes invested in the product's success and resists negative information. Documented across Zenza bondholder chats.

CheckIs the reward from product sales or from recruitment? If the money comes from new people, not new products, it is MLM.

Media gatekeeping

What you hearDo not talk to press. Only designated spokespeople can speak. Any inquiry goes to headquarters.

What it doesControls the narrative by controlling who talks. Prevents holders from comparing notes with journalists who might check registries. Channeled messaging avoids conflicting statements.

CheckIf they control who speaks, they control what gets said. A legitimate issuer welcomes press inquiries.

"Dead cat" redirect

What you hearBad news appears. The same day or next, a new project, partnership, or manga chapter is announced.

What it doesBuries bad news under a shiny announcement. The new thing gets the attention while the bad news remains unanswered. Repeatedly observed after SEC questions and CertiK badge concerns.

CheckSpot the bad news. Then check if anyone answered it. The announcement is not an answer.

Token swap confusion

What you hearProduct name changes. RYO becomes RYO-CHAN. The old token is swapped for a new one. Terms change with each swap.

What it doesResets the timeline. Old holders with losses are told the new token will fix everything. New holders enter without knowing the old project. Each swap creates confusion about what you actually own.

CheckTrace the timeline. Name changes and swaps are not fixes. What happened to the old token holders?

Synthetic market activity

What you hearA token looks actively traded. Order books show constant buy and sell walls. Volume appears real.

What it doesCreates the illusion of liquid market. In reality, the market maker is a programmed facade with no real inventory. Walls auto-restock infinitely. The appearance of trading is a stage prop to attract buyers.

CheckLook for pattern behavior: do order book walls behave like real inventory or like a script? Do prices move on real volume or stay stuck in a programmed range? A real market maker takes directional risk. A synthetic one does not.

Coordinated press amplification

What you hearHundreds of identical press releases appear across multiple news sites. The same announcement is distributed through multiple channels simultaneously.

What it doesCreates the impression of widespread news coverage and institutional interest. Paid press releases are designed to look like independent journalism. The volume of distribution substitutes for the credibility any single outlet would require.

CheckCheck if the coverage is journalism or a paid press release. Look at PR Times or similar distribution platforms. If every outlet carries the exact same text, it is a coordinated distribution, not independent reporting.

Geo-cloaking and selective accessibility

What you hearA website blocks visitors from certain countries. The same site is fully accessible from others. Content shown to Japanese visitors differs from what US visitors see.

What it doesControls which regulators, journalists, and victims can access marketing claims simultaneously. By selectively blocking access from high-risk jurisdictions, the operator maintains different narratives for different audiences. A visitor from Japan sees one version; a US IP address gets blocked or redirected. This makes independent verification harder because the evidence looks different depending on where you stand.

CheckAccess the site from different IP locations or use archived snapshots. Verify that what you see is the same content available to everyone. If the site behaves differently depending on your location, ask why.
